01 // Context

The design wasn't the problem. The accumulation was.

MotoShare is a peer-to-peer motorcycle rental marketplace in the Netherlands — connecting owners who want to monetise idle bikes with riders who want European roads without the ownership. The model works. The product, at v1, worked less well.

The founding team were motorcycle enthusiasts, not product designers. They shipped a v1 that proved demand and attracted early adopters. But early adopters forgive friction. Mainstream users don't. Scaling past the initial audience required a product that could convert browsers into renters without requiring patience for a confusing interface.

The Hypothesis

Usability improvements would directly drive conversion rate, and conversion would unlock growth. The constraint: fix the UX through frontend changes only — no backend modifications.

The Brief

Full redesign. Frontend only.

Overhaul the UI and UX across all major flows — rental booking, owner listing, payments — without touching the backend. Prove the impact through measured outcomes.

The Stakes

Every conversion point was revenue.

The business had enough traffic to measure outcomes immediately and precisely. A shallow redesign that moved nothing in the numbers would cost four months with nothing to show for it.

02 // The Problem

Three flows, all broken in different ways

The rental booking flow dropped users at multiple decision points. The owner-side listing experience had inconsistent form patterns that created uncertainty about what information was required. The payment and confirmation flow had no clear visual hierarchy guiding users through the final critical steps.

Each flow had been designed in isolation, at different times, by different hands. Nothing was catastrophically wrong in any single screen — but the accumulation of small inconsistencies created an experience that never felt trustworthy. And in a marketplace where users hand over motorcycle keys or credit cards, trust is the only thing that matters.

04 // Discovery

Research before pixels — every time

I started with the flows, not the screens. The first two weeks were documentation: mapping every business process in the product — how a user found and booked a bike, how an owner listed and managed their vehicle, how money moved between parties. These as-is flow diagrams immediately surfaced where the design had made assumptions the product couldn't support.

Then I talked to users. Surveys gave me breadth — quantitative signal on where people dropped off and what confused them. Interviews gave me the reasons behind the numbers. I spoke with both sides of the marketplace: renters who completed bookings, renters who abandoned, and owners who listed motorcycles. The patterns that emerged were consistent enough to act on without ambiguity.

Quantitative

05 // Key Insights

What the research surfaced

  • Users abandoned at motorcycle selection — not at payment. Confusion happened earlier than the founders expected. The listing cards didn't give users enough confidence to choose.

  • Owners were uncertain what information was required. Inconsistent form field labelling and no mandatory/optional distinction led to incomplete listings and owner frustration.

  • The booking flow had no progress indication. Users had no sense of how many steps remained, which created anxiety at the highest-commitment moment in the journey.

  • Visual hierarchy worked against the primary action. On multiple screens, the most important CTAs were competing visually with secondary information and navigation elements.

  • Trust signals were present but invisible. Reviews, owner verification, insurance details — all there, all buried or inconsistently applied across the booking funnel.

06 // Strategy

Fix the hierarchy. Close the leaks. Build trust where it counts.

Rather than redesigning from scratch, I prioritised changes by potential conversion impact. The highest-leverage interventions: restructuring the visual hierarchy on listing cards, adding progress indication to the checkout flow, surfacing trust signals at the specific moments users needed reassurance, and standardising the owner listing form patterns.

The approach was deliberate about what not to change. Backend-constrained decisions — pricing structure, insurance model, matching algorithm — stayed off the table. I focused entirely on the layer I could control: what users saw, how information was structured, and what they were asked to do at each step.

07 // Key Decisions

Three decisions that shaped the outcome

01

Listing Cards vs. Full Search Page

Chosen: Listing Cards First

The highest drop-off was at motorcycle selection. Redesigning the full search page would have taken 6+ weeks. Improving the card design — information hierarchy, imagery, trust signals — could ship in two weeks and be tested immediately.

Card redesign validated via A/B test — justified full search page investment in next sprint

02

Single Form vs. Step Wizard

Chosen: Step-by-Step Rental Wizard

The original single-page booking form created decision paralysis. Users couldn't tell how much work was ahead. Breaking it into a stepped wizard with clear progress indication reduced cognitive load and made the commitment feel manageable.

Wizard completions significantly higher than original form — confirmed post-launch

03

A/B Test Breadth vs. Depth

Chosen: Deep Testing on 3 High-Impact Modules

Rather than testing everything at low statistical confidence, I selected the three modules with the clearest conversion impact — listing card, booking wizard entry, confirmation step — and ran proper tests on each with sufficient traffic and time.

Each test produced a clear winner with high confidence. All three incorporated into the final design.

08 // Solution

Hundreds of screens. One consistent system.

The final design deliverable was a complete, production-ready responsive design covering every state, flow, and edge case. Desktop, mobile, and tablet — each breakpoint designed individually, not just shrunk from the desktop version.

The visual system unified what had been a fragmented collection of ad-hoc patterns into a coherent design language: a consistent type scale, a clear hierarchy of UI components, standardised form patterns, and a set of trust signal components applied consistently across the booking funnel.

09 // Impact

The numbers came back strong

3× Conversion rate across all major flows
400+ Responsive screens covering all states and edge cases
0 Backend changes required — pure frontend UX impact

Post-launch, the conversion improvement was measured across every major flow — renting, booking, and payments. The results validated the original hypothesis: fixing the design layer moved the business metric. The founders used the outcome to make the case for continued investment in design as a growth lever, not just a cost centre.

Secondary Outcome

The redesign surfaced weak points that went beyond design — specific flows where backend constraints were creating UX problems only engineering could solve. This intelligence directly informed the next product development roadmap.

10 // Reflection

What this project taught me

The constraint was the lesson

Frontend-only changes were initially frustrating. In hindsight, it was the best forcing function I've ever had. It forced me to distinguish between design problems and problems masquerading as design problems but actually rooted in product architecture. That clarity made my recommendations sharper and my prioritisation more defensible.

What I'd do differently

Instrument before redesigning

We had to wait for post-launch data to validate what research had already told us. Earlier instrumentation — event-level tracking, funnel analytics, session recording — would have enabled faster iteration and caught edge cases before launch.

What I'd expand

Build a testing culture

We tested three modules deeply. With more time, I'd have made A/B testing the default mode for any UI decision above a risk threshold — not a special activity reserved for the highest-stakes screens.

11 // AI Layer

A pre-AI project — and what that means

MotoShare predates the current generation of AI design tools. Research synthesis was done manually. Ideation happened in in-person workshops. Competitor analysis was done screen by screen, note by note. The work that took days then — interview synthesis, pattern extraction from user sessions, generating design alternatives — takes hours now.

If I ran this project today, Claude would handle research synthesis. Figma AI would accelerate alternative generation. Hotjar AI would surface session patterns automatically. The human decision-making — which direction to pursue, what trade-offs to make, what to test — would remain mine. The machine handles the processing work that used to consume the time available for thinking.

The judgment demonstrated in this case study is the same judgment I bring to AI-augmented work today. The tools that amplify it have changed. The design thinking behind them hasn't.
